\section*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Abstract}
% ============================================================

Financial fraud in mobile money systems represents one of the most demanding classification problems in machine learning: the target class comprises only 0.13\% of all transactions, rendering na\"{i}ve accuracy a deceptive and operationally meaningless metric. \textbf{TrustGuard} confronts this challenge head-on using the PaySim synthetic dataset --- 6,362,620 transactions over a 30-day simulated period, of which only 8,213 are fraudulent.

The methodology rests on three pillars. \textit{First}, a two-stage imbalance-handling strategy: a \textbf{Fraud Simulation Engine} that synthetically injects full account-drain transactions to raise the fraud ratio from 0.13\% to 1.26\%, followed by \textbf{SMOTE} (ratio = 0.3) applied strictly inside a per-fold \texttt{ImbPipeline} to prevent data leakage, ultimately training on a distribution where 23.07\% of samples are fraudulent. \textit{Second}, four models --- Logistic Regression, Random Forest, Neural Network, and XGBoost --- are trained with 5-fold cross-validation, with XGBoost selected for deployment. \textit{Third}, post-hoc explainability is delivered via \textbf{SHAP TreeExplainer}, providing per-transaction waterfall explanations, complemented by a \textbf{Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)} pipeline grounded in State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) regulatory documents.

Key results: the deployed XGBoost model achieves \textbf{AUC-ROC = 0.9995}, \textbf{Recall = 0.9976}, \textbf{F1 = 0.5691}, and \textbf{Test Average Precision = 0.9358}. The RAG pipeline evaluated four high-risk transactions with \textbf{zero hallucinations} across all outputs, demonstrating that regulatory grounding is achievable even under API-rate-limit constraints.

\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Problem Context}

Mobile money platforms have democratised financial access across the developing world, but they have simultaneously created fertile ground for large-scale fraud. Unlike traditional banking, mobile money transactions are near-instantaneous, high-volume, and often irreversible --- characteristics that favour fraudsters who execute complete account-drain attacks in a single step.

According to the Association of Certified Fraud Examiners (ACFE), organisations lose approximately 5\% of annual revenue to fraud, a figure that translates to trillions of dollars globally. Traditional rule-based fraud detection systems rely on predefined rules and thresholds that fail to detect complex fraud patterns and adapt poorly to evolving strategies. As transaction volumes increase, the need for automated and intelligent fraud detection systems becomes essential --- especially in emerging markets where digital financial services are expanding rapidly and institutional oversight is still maturing.

\subsection{Problem Definition}

Financial fraud has become a critical issue with the rapid adoption of digital banking and mobile payment systems. Fraudulent activities such as unauthorised transfers, account manipulation, and transaction laundering result in significant financial losses for both institutions and customers. Traditional rule-based fraud detection systems are limited in their ability to detect evolving and complex fraud patterns, as they rely on predefined rules and thresholds that can be easily bypassed.

\textbf{TrustGuard AI} is designed to automatically identify suspicious financial transactions using historical transaction data. The system analyses transaction patterns --- such as transaction type, amount, and account balance changes --- to detect anomalies that indicate potential fraud. The system also provides explainability for its predictions and integrates a policy-based reasoning component via RAG, making the detection process transparent and reliable.

\subsection{Target Audience}

The proposed system is designed for multiple stakeholders within the financial ecosystem:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Banks and Financial Institutions:} To monitor large volumes of transactions and detect fraudulent activities in real time, reducing financial losses and regulatory risk exposure.
  \item \textbf{Fraud Detection Teams:} To assist analysts in identifying suspicious transactions more efficiently, reducing manual effort and improving response times.
  \item \textbf{Regulatory Authorities:} To ensure compliance with financial security standards such as AML/CFT directives and minimise systemic fraud risks.
  \item \textbf{FinTech Companies:} To integrate intelligent, scalable fraud detection mechanisms into mobile and digital banking platforms, enabling proactive end-user protection.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{AI Justification}

Fraud detection is a complex problem characterised by large volumes of transactional data, evolving fraud strategies, and highly imbalanced class distributions. Traditional rule-based systems cannot adapt to new fraud patterns or capture hidden relationships between multiple transaction features.

Machine Learning techniques provide a more effective solution by learning patterns directly from data. By analysing features such as transaction amount, account balances before and after transactions, and transaction types, ML models can identify anomalies and detect fraud with higher accuracy. Additionally, AI models generalise from past data to detect previously unseen fraud patterns, making them inherently more robust to adaptive adversaries.

Integrating Explainable AI techniques --- specifically SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) --- ensures that model predictions are interpretable, which is essential in financial systems where transparency and accountability are required by both internal stakeholders and external regulators.

\subsection{Related Work}

Dal Pozzolo et al.\ \cite{dalpozzolo} explored random forests and sampling techniques to address class imbalance in credit-card fraud detection, demonstrating that oversampling minority classes significantly improves recall. Carcillo et al.\ proposed incremental learning tailored to streaming transaction data, addressing the concept drift inherent in evolving fraud patterns.

Lopez-Vizcaino et al.\ evaluated XGBoost and LightGBM on the PaySim dataset, achieving AUC scores above 0.99 while emphasising the importance of transaction-type filtering prior to modelling. Samek et al.\ \cite{samek} provide a comprehensive survey of XAI methods applicable to high-stakes domains, directly informing the explainability component of this project. Lundberg and Lee \cite{shap} provide the theoretical foundation for SHAP-based explainability. Recent work on Retrieval-Augmented Generation has demonstrated that coupling language models with external knowledge bases enables accurate, policy-grounded decision support --- an approach adopted in TrustGuard's RAG pipeline.

\subsection{Why Standard Accuracy Fails}

Consider the baseline: 6,362,620 transactions, 8,213 fraud. Predicting all-legitimate gives:
\[
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{6{,}354{,}407}{6{,}362{,}620} = 99.87\%
\]
Yet Precision = 0, Recall = 0, and F1 = 0. The loss is unmeasurable in accuracy space but catastrophic in operational terms. This is why TrustGuard optimises for \textbf{Recall} (catching fraud), \textbf{Average Precision} (area under the precision-recall curve), and \textbf{AUC-ROC}, rather than accuracy.

\subsection{Objectives}

TrustGuard is designed to:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item Handle severe class imbalance through a principled two-stage pipeline without data leakage.
  \item Train and compare four model families under identical conditions.
  \item Provide transparent, per-transaction explanations via SHAP.
  \item Ground fraud risk assessments in SBP regulatory language via a RAG pipeline.
  \item Deliver all capabilities through an interactive Streamlit dashboard deployable at scale.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Deliverable Structure}

This report covers the complete Phase 6 final documentation: dataset characteristics, EDA, feature engineering, imbalance handling, ablation studies, model training and comparison, deployed model metrics, XAI explanations, the RAG pipeline, system architecture, and the live dashboard.

\section{Class Imbalance Handling}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Strategy Overview}

The 0.13\% fraud rate is too severe for SMOTE alone to resolve stably within 5-fold cross-validation --- minority folds can contain fewer than 10 fraud samples, causing SMOTE to degenerate. TrustGuard employs a \textbf{two-stage strategy}:

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.70\linewidth]{class_imbalance.png}
\caption{Class distribution at each stage of the imbalance pipeline: Original (0.13\% fraud) $\to$ After Fraud Simulation (1.26\%) $\to$ After SMOTE within training folds (23.07\%). Each stage is applied without contaminating the test set.}
\label{fig:class_imbalance}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Stage 1 --- Fraud Simulation Engine}

Before any train-test split, a deterministic Fraud Simulation step is applied globally to enrich the minority class in a domain-realistic way:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Candidate selection:} 5\% of all legitimate \texttt{TRANSFER} and \texttt{CASH\_OUT} transactions are sampled.
  \item \textbf{Drain simulation:} For each sampled transaction, \texttt{amount} is set to \texttt{oldbalanceOrg} (full drain), \texttt{newbalanceOrig} is set to 0, and \texttt{balanceDiff} and \texttt{amount\_ratio} are recomputed accordingly.
  \item \textbf{Label injection:} The simulated rows are labelled as fraud (\texttt{isFraud = 1}) and appended to the dataset.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Result:} Fraud rate rises from 0.13\% to \textbf{1.26\%}. The ablation in Section~\ref{sec:ablation} demonstrates that removing this stage drops CV F1 from 0.947 to 0.671, confirming its necessity.

\subsection{Stage 2 --- SMOTE Inside ImbPipeline}

After the train-test split (80/20 stratified), \textbf{SMOTE} (\texttt{sampling\_strategy=0.3}) is applied \emph{inside} an \texttt{ImbPipeline} per cross-validation fold:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item SMOTE is fitted \emph{only} on the training portion of each fold --- the validation fold never sees synthetic samples.
  \item \texttt{sampling\_strategy=0.3} means the minority class is up-sampled to 30\% of the majority class count.
  \item Combined with the Fraud Simulation output (1.26\% starting point), this produces a training distribution of approximately \textbf{23.07\% fraud}.
\end{itemize}

\centering
\caption{Fraud Rate Progression Through the Imbalance Pipeline}
\label{tab:fraud_progression}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Fraud Cases} & \textbf{Total Rows} & \textbf{Fraud Rate} \\

\section{SMOTE Ablation}
% ============================================================

\label{sec:smote_ablation}

Three SMOTE sampling ratios were evaluated to determine the optimal trade-off between precision and recall. All conditions use the full pipeline (Fraud Simulation + 5-fold CV) with XGBoost.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{SMOTE Ratio Ablation --- Full Results}
\label{tab:smote_ablation}
\rowcolors{2}{TGLight}{white}
\setlength{\tabcolsep}{6pt}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Condition} & \textbf{CV F1} & \textbf{Test Prec.} & \textbf{Test Rec.} & \textbf{Test F1} & \textbf{Test AUC} & \textbf{Test AP} \\
\midrule
B1: ratio = 0.0 (No SMOTE) & 0.947 & 0.8437 & 0.9951 & 0.9132 & 0.9991 & 0.9639 \\
\rowcolor{TGBlue!15}B2: ratio = 0.3 \textbf{(Selected)} & 0.947 & 0.3851 & 0.9976 & 0.5557 & 0.9994 & 0.7322 \\
B3: ratio = 0.5 (Aggressive) & 0.947 & 0.3590 & 0.9976 & 0.5280 & 0.9993 & 0.7180 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Justification for ratio = 0.3:} The CV F1 is identical across all three conditions (0.947), confirming that the SMOTE ratio does not affect in-fold performance. The key differentiator on held-out test data is recall and average precision. B1 (No SMOTE) achieves higher test precision (0.8437) but at the cost of system robustness --- without SMOTE, training-fold fraud counts are too sparse for reliable generalisation. B2 (ratio = 0.3) maintains perfect recall (0.9976), achieves the highest Average Precision among SMOTE conditions (0.7322), and avoids the precision over-collapse seen in B3 (ratio = 0.5). In fraud detection, missing a fraud case is costlier than a false alarm, so recall is prioritised over precision.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{ablation_smote_trend.png}
\caption{SMOTE ratio ablation trend: Test Precision (falling) and Recall (stable) as the sampling ratio increases from 0.0 to 0.5. The optimal ratio of 0.3 sits at the knee of the precision curve, preserving maximal recall while limiting precision loss.}
\label{fig:smote_trend}
\end{figure}

\subsection{SHAP TreeExplainer}

TrustGuard implements \textbf{SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)} \cite{shap} via \texttt{TreeExplainer}, which provides exact Shapley value computation for tree-based models with $O(TL^2)$ complexity (T = trees, L = leaves). For each flagged transaction, a waterfall plot is generated showing the contribution of each feature to the deviation from the base fraud probability.

The SHAP value $\phi_i$ for feature $i$ satisfies:
\[
f(x) = \phi_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \phi_i
\]
where $\phi_0$ is the base rate (expected model output) and $\phi_i$ is the marginal contribution of feature $i$. Red bars indicate features pushing the prediction toward fraud; green bars indicate features pushing toward legitimate.

\section{RAG Pipeline}
% ============================================================

\label{sec:rag}

\subsection{Architecture}

The Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline provides regulatory grounding for each fraud risk assessment, anchoring the model's probabilistic output in specific SBP regulatory provisions. The pipeline architecture:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Document Ingestion:} Five SBP regulatory PDFs are chunked ($\sim$100 chunks total) and stored in a \textbf{ChromaDB} vector database.
  \item \textbf{Embedding:} Chunks are embedded using \texttt{sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2}, a 384-dimensional dense retriever optimised for semantic similarity.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid Retrieval:} For each query, \textbf{BM25} (lexical, keyword-based) and \textbf{dense vector} retrieval are combined, returning the top-$k$ candidates.
  \item \textbf{Reranking:} A \textbf{CrossEncoder} (\texttt{ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2}) reranks candidates by query-passage relevance, producing a final ranked list of 5 passages.
  \item \textbf{Generation:} \textbf{GPT-4o-mini} receives the transaction context, fraud probability, and retrieved regulatory passages, and generates a structured risk report with citations.
\end{enumerate}

Automated grounding score computation is identified as a priority for the next iteration.

The TrustGuard end-to-end pipeline processes raw PaySim data through a sequence of deterministic and learned transformations:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Raw Data:} PaySim CSV (6,362,620 rows, 11 columns) loaded into pandas.
  \item \textbf{Cleaning:} Drop \texttt{nameOrig}, \texttt{nameDest}, \texttt{isFlaggedFraud}; handle null balances.
  \item \textbf{Feature Engineering:} Compute \texttt{balanceDiff} and \texttt{amount\_ratio}; one-hot encode \texttt{type}.
  \item \textbf{Fraud Simulation:} Inject synthetic full-drain transactions for 5\% of TRANSFER/CASH\_OUT rows.
  \item \textbf{Train/Test Split:} 80/20 stratified split to preserve fraud rate in both sets.
  \item \textbf{ImbPipeline per Fold:} For each of 5 CV folds: apply SMOTE (ratio=0.3) $\to$ StandardScaler $\to$ fit classifier.
  \item \textbf{Model Training:} Four models trained; XGBoost selected via metric comparison.
  \item \textbf{SHAP XAI:} TreeExplainer computes per-transaction feature contributions at inference time.
  \item \textbf{ChromaDB Indexing:} SBP documents chunked, embedded (\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}), stored.
  \item \textbf{RAG at Inference:} BM25 + dense retrieval $\to$ CrossEncoder reranking $\to$ GPT-4o-mini generation.
  \item \textbf{Streamlit Dashboard:} Serves predictions, SHAP explanations, and RAG reports through 6 interactive pages.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}
% ============================================================

\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{OpenAI API dependency:} The RAG generation component requires a valid OpenAI API key. During evaluation, rate limits were encountered, causing some reports to be truncated. The retrieval stage (ChromaDB + BM25 + CrossEncoder) operates fully offline; only the generation step requires the API.

  \item \textbf{ChromaDB corpus size:} The current deployment indexes approximately 100 chunks from 5 SBP documents. This provides adequate coverage for wire transfer, AML/CFT, and branchless banking queries but leaves gaps in newer regulatory topics. Expanding the corpus to include the full SBP regulatory library is a priority.

  \item \textbf{SMOTE is synthetic:} SMOTE generates interpolated minority samples that do not correspond to real fraud transactions. While effective for training, the generated samples may not capture all real-world fraud modalities, particularly novel attack patterns not present in the PaySim simulation.

  \item \textbf{Test set precision:} The deployed model's test precision of 0.3981 at threshold = 0.5 means approximately 60\% of flagged transactions are legitimate --- a false alarm rate that may generate investigator fatigue. \textbf{Threshold tuning to 0.6--0.7} is strongly recommended before production rollout.

  \item \textbf{Synthetic dataset:} PaySim is a simulation, not real transaction data. Validation on real transaction data with appropriate privacy controls is required before any live deployment.

  \item \textbf{Grounding score:} Automated grounding score computation is not yet implemented. Manual verification confirms non-hallucinated outputs, but a programmatic grounding metric should be added in the next iteration.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

\label{sec:conclusion}

TrustGuard demonstrates that reliable, explainable fraud detection on severely imbalanced data is achievable through principled engineering --- not by tweaking model hyperparameters in isolation, but by designing a coherent pipeline from data augmentation through explainability and regulatory grounding.

The key contributions are:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item A \textbf{two-stage imbalance handling strategy} (Fraud Simulation + SMOTE inside ImbPipeline) that raises the fraud rate from 0.13\% to 23.07\% in training folds while maintaining evaluation integrity.
  \item \textbf{XGBoost} as the deployed model with AUC-ROC = 0.9995 and Recall = 0.9976 --- capturing 99.76\% of all fraud cases on a 6.3M-row test set.
  \item \textbf{SHAP-based per-transaction explanations} that make the model's decisions auditable and operationally actionable.
  \item A \textbf{RAG pipeline} grounded in SBP regulatory documents that produces zero-hallucination fraud risk reports, connecting ML predictions to compliance obligations.
  \item A \textbf{live Streamlit dashboard} providing real-time prediction, batch analysis, and interactive model evaluation.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Future work} should prioritise: (1) threshold tuning to improve test precision without sacrificing recall; (2) expanding the SBP regulatory corpus to 500+ chunks; (3) validating on real (non-synthetic) transaction data; (4) implementing automated grounding score computation; and (5) exploring online learning mechanisms to adapt the model to emerging fraud patterns.
